# **It's Not What You Say. It's Not How You Say It. It's *Why*.**

### **Introducing GAME—a personality test for people who want to understand the rules before they get played.**

---

There's a phrase I've heard my entire life that has always made me raise an eyebrow.

*"It's not what you say, it's how you say it."*

I understand the instinct. Tone matters. Delivery matters. Fair enough. But this framing is like a banana peel to me—the slick, ubiquitous, takes-you-down-before-you-know-what-happened kind. Because the *how* is still downstream of something more fundamental. Something most people never ask, and no personality quiz I ever tried actually measures.

**The why.**

Why are you saying this, or not? What are you actually trying to do, or get? What is the game you're playing — and do you even know you're playing one?

Intentions matter. More than ever, especially in a world where Artificial Intelligence, Augmented Illusions, Accelerated Idiocracy, and Authoritarian Indoctrination all thrive on the same feed. Clarity is underrated. Being aware of respective motivations — yours, mine, theirs — is the key to understanding each other. The condition for playing fair. The *why* is the key to trust, and trust the key to anything worth building together.

We live in a world where social is commercial and appearances sell more than substance. Robert Greene's *Art of Seduction* is a bestseller. Seduction as calculated strategy, manipulation as self-help. As if love weren't an infinite game of connection... but(t) I digress.

Tom Waits knows better. *"There's no prayer like desire,"* he sings. I wrote this thing about it, amongst many other things:

*Truth ain't a look, or a badge you wear,* *It's the why behind how you even care.* *Not how you dance, or what you do,* *But why you whisper "I love you, too."*

*There's no prayer like desire,* *Why we rise higher, on fire.*

**Why** is the question that changed how I read people. How I write lyrics about the world gone bananas. How I learn, and grow. It’s the question at the heart of what matters most—the secret ingredient of desire. 

There’s no magic recipe for peace and harmony. And that game of desire? It sure is worth aiming for. But(t) it always depends. It depends on its players… So, I made a thing, a GAME framework of sorts—using the interpersonal taxonomy I wish I'd had twenty years ago, before I learned its lessons the hard way.

---

## **The problem with personality tests**

You've taken the tests. MBTI tells you you're an INFJ or an ENTP. The Enneagram says you're a 4 with a 3 wing. Big Five gives you a percentile score on openness. StrengthsFinder tells you you're an "Ideator" or a "Relator."

These are all, in their own way, maps of *the how*—how we process information, how we communicate, how we prefer to work, how we show up in a room. They're useful. I'm not dismissing them.

But they share a blind spot.

None of them seriously ask: **what do you actually want from the game?** What motivates you to play, or pray, with or to others?

Not what you think you want. Not what you tell yourself you want. What you actually want, deep down—when resources are limited, when credit is on the line, when you're tired or scared or ambitious or hurt. 

That's the primal question at the heart of intention. And intention is where the real game is played. 

So I mapped it into four types—four intentions, one word: GAME. Not a judgment, not a ranking. A mirror. A framework for those who'd rather see the game clearly than get played by it.

---

## **Being kind is being clear**

Before I describe the four types, I want to say something about what this framework is and isn't.

It is not a judgment. It is not a ranking of good people and bad people. It is not a moral hierarchy.

It is an awareness tool.

I've come to believe—from experience—that **being kind is being clear**. Not being nice. *Clear.* Nice is comfortable. Nice is smooth. But(t) sometimes, nice is also a form of self-serving cowardice dressed in good manners. Clarity is the harder, braver thing: sharing what's actually happening, naming it. It’s about choosing the long game. Reality over fantasy.

Because not everyone is playing the same game as you. And the sooner you know that — really know it, not just suspect it — the sooner you can stop being surprised by it. The four types that follow aren't about labeling people. They're about finally being able to read the room.

Because knowledge and growth always start with awareness. And awareness, it turns out, starts with asking why.

## **The four types — G.A.M.E.**

Ok. Ready to G.A.M.E? Here's the taxonomy. My map shows four core personality types, defined entirely by their desire, their *intention*—i.e. what they're fundamentally trying to do, in any given interaction or relationship.

### **G — The Grabber**

*Finite game · Zero-sum · The Wolf*

The Grabber operates from a deep, often unconscious belief that every interaction has a winner and a loser. Resources — attention, credit, status, energy, money, love—are limited, and the job is to get yours before someone else does.

This doesn't always look predatory. In fact, Grabbers are often charming. And many are charismatic leaders, who genuinely believe they're building something important. The tell is what happens when things get tight: whose interests suddenly become the only ones that matter?

The Grabber's charm is deployed instrumentally. Their empathy is a targeting system—performed when useful, absent when not. Their generosity, when it exists, is transactional, calculated. It is primarily an investment with expected returns. None of this is necessarily conscious, by the way... The most sophisticated Grabbers are also the ones who have most successfully convinced themselves (and others) that they're something else entirely\! 

The world needs some Grabber energy. Competitive drive, tactical clarity, the ability to protect what you've built—these are real capacities. The problem is that in modern society, unchecked, Grabbers tend to deplete the ecosystems they prey on. They play finite games only. And bet on getting away with it. Unlike Blaise Pascal warned, they believe the end justifies the means. For Grabbers, winning means rules be damned.

The good news is they can only do this for as long as someone lets them.

---

### **A — The Aligner**

*Conditional game · Tit-for-tat · The Mirror*

The Aligner is the personality type that game theorists have been implicitely modeling for decades. In the famous iterated Prisoner's Dilemma—the mathematical model of cooperation—the winning strategy wasn't aggressive, it wasn't purely generous, it wasn't cynical. It was *tit-for-tat*: cooperate first, then mirror whatever the other player does.

The Aligner lives this instinctively. They are generous by default, firm when exploited, and they have a remarkably precise internal ledger. They don't moralize. They don't punish dramatically. They just... adjust. Quietly, accurately, without ceremony.

Their empathy is real but analytical—less a feeling they're overcome by, more a capacity they deploy to read situations accurately. This can make them seem cold to people who mistake emotional display for emotional depth.

The Aligner's great strength is that they are nearly impossible to exploit for long. They notice, they calibrate, they stop. The Aligner's shadow is rigidity dressed as fairness: when the ledger becomes the point, relationships stop being alive. Sometimes the right move is to give more than you've received—not as a failure of accounting, but as a bet on what's possible. 

---

### **M — The Multiplier**

*Infinite game · Abundance · The Gardener*

The Multiplier is the rarest type, and the one whose operating logic is hardest to explain to people who haven't experienced it.

They genuinely believe—not as a philosophy but as a felt reality—that 1+1=3. That the game expands when you're generous on it. That trust is a renewable resource, not a depleting one. That investing in someone else's growth doesn't subtract from your own.

This is not naivety. Or rather, it starts as naivety and becomes, through experience, a sophisticated systems understanding. The Multiplier has usually lived long enough to see the compounding returns of genuine generosity—the networks built by connecting people without agenda, the institutions transformed by leaders who actually enjoy empowering others, and watching them grow, win-win style.

The Multiplier plays what philosopher James Carse called the infinite game: the goal is not to win but to keep the game going, to expand what's possible, to bring more players in rather than eliminate them.

Their shadow is what I would call naive exposure. A Multiplier without Aligner instincts is a generous person in a world full of Grabbers—which means they are, at some point, going to get fouled, and burned… 

The solution is not to become less generous. God forbid. It's to develop a spine alongside the generosity. Because no one can pour from an empty vessel, however noble the cause.

---

### **E — The Easer**

*Self-negating game · Sheeple · The Martyr*

The Easer is the type most likely to read this and think: *oh, that's not me, I'm just a kind person.*

Which is, almost exactly, the thing an Easer would think.

The Easer gives. Consistently, often invisibly, frequently at their own expense. They smooth friction. They absorb tension so others don't have to feel it. They apologize for things done to them. They stay in relationships long past the point of health because leaving feels selfish. They become the invisible glue in families, friendships, organizations—the player nobody notices until it fades, and dissolves...

Here is the uncomfortable truth about the Easer: **they are the Grabber's most necessary accomplice.**

Not from malice. Not even from weakness, exactly. From conflict-avoidance raised to a life philosophy. Every time the Easer absorbs what should be named, every time they smooth over what should be addressed, every time they make it easy for the Grabber to keep operating—they are making a choice. They are choosing the discomfort of slow self-erasure over the discomfort of a difficult conversation.

This is not kindness. It is the *appearance* of kindness in service of misguided self-protection.

The invitation to the Easer is perhaps the most urgent of any type: **your disappearance is not nobility. It is unavailability.** The world doesn't need your sacrifice of yourself. It needs your full, present, boundaried, opinionated self—the one that got so good at hiding.

## **Most of us are a blend**

Here's what the GAME quiz reveals—and what any honest self-examination confirms: most people aren't purely one type. We're a primary type with a secondary that shapes how the primary plays out.

A few hybrid portraits worth naming:

**Multiplier \+ Aligner** (*The Grounded Visionary*) : the healthiest combination, and probably the rarest. Abundance belief with clear limits. Generous and sustainable. Builds things that last.

**Multiplier \+ Easer** (*The Depleted Idealist*) : wants to generate good in the world and will sacrifice themselves to do it. Noble, and dangerous to themselves. The garden runs dry.

**Grabber \+ Multiplier** (*The Visionary Predator*) : perhaps the most interesting and most common in positions of power. The big picture framing is real. So is the self-interest underneath it. The question they rarely ask themselves: *who, exactly, am I building this for?*

**Grabber \+ Aligner** (*The Calculated Operator*) : has a code, plays by it, and the code is fundamentally about “winning”... Reliable in the sense that you know what you're getting.

**Grabber \+ Easer** (*The Conflicted Taker*) : takes, and feels terrible about it. Possibly the most exhausting combination to inhabit. Guilt-driven generosity that still somehow benefits them, followed by resentment in both directions.

**Aligner \+ Easer** (*The Burnt-Out Peacekeeper*) : knows exactly when things are off, and absorbs it anyway. The internal ledger fills. When they finally stop, everyone is confused—they seemed so measured, so fine...

## **Why this matters now**

*I built this framework because I needed it. Because we needed it.*

We've all lived on the receiving end of all four types. Most of us have been at least three of them—at different points in our lives, in different relationships, under different pressures. The Easer who enabled a Grabber for years while calling it loyalty. The Multiplier who couldn't understand why someone would take what was offered and disappear. The Aligner who let the ledger calcify into coldness.

We live in a world that rewards Grabber behavior at institutional scale—that has, in fact, built many of its most powerful belief systems around the assumption that everyone is, at bottom, a Grabber. That is Hobbes’ Wolf philosophy, and it is as anxious and bitter now as it ever was. That scarcity is the natural state. That zero-sum is just realism.

I’m more of a Jean Jacques Rousseau type of gal, tho. I don't believe that. And, even with awareness, I suspect I’ll never stop being surprised by it. But(t) I've stopped being destroyed by it. Which is a different kind of freedom…

**The WHY is the most underrated question I know.** Not "what did they do", but(t) "what were they trying to accomplish?" Not "how did they say it”, but(t) "what did they actually want from this interaction?"

Once you start asking that question, you can't stop. And once you practice, you get sharper. “It gets better” when you start to see the game. Or rather, the games. And then, you get to decide—consciously, deliberately, with your whole self—how you want to play it. Because that matters, too.
